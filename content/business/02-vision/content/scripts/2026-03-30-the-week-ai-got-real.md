## SCRIPT

---

### HOOK (0:00 - 0:30) -- 80 words

[DIRECT TO CAMERA. SERIOUS. NO SMILE. SITTING CLOSE.]

OpenAI just killed Sora. Their flagship video product. One million dollars a day in compute. Fewer than five hundred thousand users. Disney found out an hour before the public announcement and pulled a billion-dollar deal on the spot.

[PAUSE. 2 seconds.]

And the same week... a protocol nobody outside of tech has heard of crossed ninety-seven million installs. Quietly. Without a single press conference.

[LEAN IN]

That contrast... is the entire story of where AI is going.

---

### ACT 1 -- THE PRESENT (0:30 - 3:00) -- 380 words

[SLIGHTLY MORE RELAXED. CONVERSATIONAL.]

So here's what actually happened this week. And I don't mean the headlines -- I mean what the headlines are trying to hide from you.

OpenAI launched Sora six months ago. Remember the hype? "Hollywood is dead." "Anyone can make a movie now." Sam Altman on stage showing off sixty-second clips that looked like Ridley Scott directed them on acid.

[B-ROLL: montage of Sora launch hype clips, headlines, Twitter reactions]

The reality? Fifteen million dollars a day in inference costs. Total revenue from the app -- lifetime, not monthly, lifetime -- two point one million dollars. They spent more in two days than they earned in six months.

[LOOK AWAY, THEN BACK]

And Disney... Disney committed a billion dollars to a partnership with OpenAI around Sora. They found out it was being shut down less than an hour before the public did. That's not a business relationship. That's a breakup text.

[PAUSE]

Now, the same week. The Model Context Protocol -- MCP -- hit ninety-seven million installs. If you don't know what that is, think of it as USB for AI. It's the standard that lets any AI agent connect to any tool. Any database. Any platform. And every major AI company now ships with MCP support built in. OpenAI, Google, Mistral, Cohere -- all of them.

[B-ROLL: screen recording of MCP servers list, terminal showing MCP connections, tmux session with agents using tools]

Also this week. Mistral raised eight hundred and thirty million dollars. Not for a product. Not for an app. For a datacenter. Thirteen thousand eight hundred Nvidia GPUs going into a facility south of Paris. Forty-four megawatts of power. That's not a startup raising money -- that's a country building infrastructure.

[SERIOUS]

And here's what nobody is connecting: Anthropic, the company behind Claude, shipped seventy-four releases in fifty-two days. Voice mode. Computer use. Background agents. A scheduling system. They didn't announce some grand vision of the future. They just... shipped. Every single day. While everyone else was making PowerPoint decks about AGI.

I was in my terminal this week, dispatching agents through tmux panes, and it hit me -- the split is already here.

---

### ACT 2 -- THE DEEPER QUESTION (3:00 - 7:00) -- 620 words

[LEAN BACK SLIGHTLY. MORE REFLECTIVE. SLOWER PACE.]

There's a philosopher -- Martin Heidegger -- who said something in the 1950s that I think about constantly when it comes to technology. He said that a tool, when it's working properly, becomes invisible. You don't think about the hammer. You think about the nail. The tool... disappears.

[PAUSE. LET IT SIT.]

MCP is the invisible tool. It works. You don't think about it. Your AI agent connects to Postgres, reads your Slack, checks your calendar, writes the code, pushes to Git -- and at no point do you stop to think about the protocol making all of that possible. It just... works. Like HTTP. Like TCP/IP. Like electricity in the wall.

Sora was the opposite. Sora was the spectacle. It was designed to be seen. To make you say "wow." To go viral on Twitter. And it did. For about three weeks. And then everyone moved on, because spectacle doesn't solve problems. Spectacle entertains. And entertainment, in technology, has a very short shelf life.

[B-ROLL: side-by-side of a Sora-generated video clip vs. a terminal with agents working quietly]

Here's where this gets interesting. This isn't just about AI. This is about... us. About what we value.

[LOOK AWAY THEN BACK. MORE PERSONAL.]

We're obsessed with the spectacular. With the demo. With the thing that makes us feel something for thirty seconds. And we ignore the infrastructure. We ignore the plumbing. Nobody posts about plumbing on Instagram. But plumbing is the reason your city works.

The internet itself followed this exact pattern. In the nineties, everyone was building flashy websites with spinning GIFs and auto-playing MIDI music. Remember that? The companies that survived weren't the flashiest. They were the ones that built protocols. TCP/IP. HTTP. DNS. Boring, invisible, absolutely foundational.

[LEAN IN]

We're at that exact moment with AI right now. The Sora era -- the "look at this cool demo" era -- it's ending. Not slowly. This week, it ended with a thud. A million-dollar-a-day thud.

And what's replacing it is what always replaces spectacle: infrastructure.

[PAUSE]

Mistral isn't building the flashiest AI model. They're building a datacenter. Anthropic isn't making the prettiest demos. They're shipping developer tools. Seventy-four releases. Not seventy-four announcements -- seventy-four releases. Real code. Real tools. Real infrastructure that real people use to build real things.

[SERIOUS]

And there's something else. Mistral Small 4 just came out -- an open-source model that beats commercial models three to five times its size. Voxtral, their text-to-speech model? Open-source, four billion parameters, runs on a laptop, and in blind tests it matches or beats ElevenLabs. The French army is deploying Mistral models for sovereign AI. Forty-eight percent of French people now use generative AI.

[B-ROLL: Mistral Small 4 benchmark comparisons, Voxtral demo]

This isn't Paris trying to copy Silicon Valley. This is a fundamentally different approach. Open-source. Sovereign. Infrastructure-first. And it's working.

The philosophical point is this: we're watching, in real time, the market correct for spectacle. The market is saying, very clearly, very loudly -- we don't need another cool demo. We need things that work. We need plumbing. We need the invisible tools.

And if you understand that... you understand where the opportunity is.

---

### ACT 3 -- THE CAIO PERSPECTIVE (7:00 - 12:00) -- 720 words

[SIT FORWARD. MORE ENERGY. THIS IS THE VALUE SECTION.]

Alright. So what do you actually do with this? Because philosophical frameworks are great, but I'm a builder. I need to build things. So let's talk about what's real.

[DIRECT]

First: the AI video market is not dead. Sora dying doesn't mean AI-generated video is over. It means standalone AI video apps are over. The winner in AI video won't be a separate product -- it'll be a feature inside something else. Inside editing software. Inside marketing tools. Inside game engines. That's the infrastructure play. Don't build the spectacle. Build the pipe it flows through.

[PAUSE]

Second: the opportunity right now is in agent orchestration. And I don't say that as a theorist. I say that as someone who spent this week dispatching two hundred and sixty-seven specialized AI agents through a terminal.

[B-ROLL: screen recording of tmux session with multiple agents running, terminal showing agent dispatch commands, split panes with parallel work happening]

Here's what my week looked like. I have a system -- call it an AI operating system -- where Claude Code controls my screen, takes voice commands, runs background agents that loop every few hours checking on tasks, and connects to every tool I use through MCP. One person. Doing the work of what would have been a ten-person team three years ago.

And that's not a flex. That's the point. The solo founder advantage right now is... almost unfair. One person with the right AI infrastructure can move at startup velocity. Not "kinda like a startup." Actual startup velocity. Shipping features daily. Running parallel workstreams. Deploying to production while you're eating dinner.

[LEAN IN]

So here are the concrete opportunities I see right now.

One: AI consulting. Companies are desperate for someone who understands how to wire AI agents into their existing systems. Not someone who can explain what GPT is -- they've got that. Someone who can actually architect agent pipelines. Connect their CRM to their AI. Build the plumbing. A CAIO -- a Chief AI Officer -- but the kind who actually builds, not the kind who makes slides.

[LOOK DIRECTLY AT CAMERA]

Two: vertical AI products built on MCP. The protocol has ninety-seven million installs and fifty-eight hundred community servers. But most industries still don't have a good AI integration. Legal. Healthcare. Real estate. Construction. Pick an industry, build the MCP server that connects their specific tools to AI agents, and you have a business. Not a sexy business. A plumbing business. And plumbing businesses print money.

Three: agent orchestration as a service. Most companies can't manage multi-agent systems. They don't know how to coordinate ten AI agents working on different parts of a problem. That's a skill. A rare one. And skills that are rare and in demand... you know how that equation ends.

[B-ROLL: diagram showing multi-agent orchestration flow, agents connecting through MCP to various tools]

Four -- and this is the big picture one: infrastructure plays. Look at what Mistral just did. Eight hundred and thirty million dollars for compute infrastructure. They're not betting on having the best model. They're betting on having the compute to serve everyone's models. That's the picks-and-shovels play. In a gold rush, sell shovels.

[PAUSE]

Now, you don't need eight hundred million dollars. But the principle scales down. Build the tools that builders need. Build the plumbing. Be the infrastructure layer. Even a small MCP server that solves one specific problem for one specific industry... that's a real business. That's recurring revenue. That's the kind of thing that compounds.

The companies that will be worth billions in five years aren't being built on stage right now. They're being built in terminals. Quietly. One commit at a time.

---

### ACT 4 -- THE FUTURE (12:00 - 14:00) -- 280 words

[MEASURED. CONFIDENT BUT NOT ARROGANT.]

So here's where I think this goes. And I'll be specific, because vague predictions are worthless.

Six months from now: MCP becomes mandatory. Like HTTPS. If your AI product doesn't speak MCP, it doesn't get adopted. Period. The protocol war is already over. MCP won.

One year from now: standalone AI products consolidate hard. Most of the AI startups you see on Product Hunt today won't exist. The ones that survive will be infrastructure providers or deep vertical plays. The "wrapper" era is truly, finally dead.

[LEAN IN]

Three years from now: we get the AI operating system. Not an app. Not a chatbot. A protocol layer that sits between you and every digital tool you use. You talk to it. It orchestrates agents. Those agents use MCP to connect to everything. And it just... works. Like the internet works. Invisibly. Heidegger's tool that disappears.

The French angle matters here. Mistral building sovereign infrastructure, open-source models that run locally, the French army deploying AI they control -- that's not nationalism. That's pragmatism. And Europe, for the first time in a long time, might actually be competitive in this race. Not by out-spending Silicon Valley. By out-building on open standards.

[DIRECT TO CAMERA]

So what do you do with this? Pick one thing. Learn MCP. Build one agent pipeline. Solve one real problem for one real industry. Don't chase the spectacle. Build the plumbing. The opportunity window is right now -- while everyone else is still chasing the next Sora.

---

### CLOSING (14:00 - 14:45) -- 130 words

[LEAN IN. QUIETER. MORE PERSONAL.]

Look... I think we're at one of those moments that we'll look back on and say, "that's when it shifted." Not because of one product dying or one protocol winning. But because the market finally started rewarding the builders over the performers.

[PAUSE]

And if you're the kind of person who'd rather build something real than chase a demo... this is your moment.

[SLIGHT SMILE]

I'm going to keep building this in public. Every week. If that's something you want to follow, you know what to do.

[HOLD EYE CONTACT. 3 SECONDS.]

See you next week.

[HOLD 2 MORE SECONDS. CUT TO BLACK.]
