1 Foreword - Why this training exists
There is a category of European executives and senior managers that no one is training properly on
artificial intelligence. They are not engineers, data scientists, or developers. They are Chief Marketing
Officers, Chief Human Resources Officers, Chiefs of Transformation, Heads of Digital, Heads of
Operations. They carry the responsibility of integrating AI into their organization, arbitrating six- or
seven-figure budgets, reporting to their exécutive committee on the state of their AI strategy - yet they
have neither the time nor the inclination to learn how to code, nor should they. That is not their job.

The market offers them two types of training. On one side, overly technical programs (Python, machine
learning, advanced prompt engineering) that do not match their rôle. On the other side, inspirational
keynotes and generic white papers that provide no concrète tooling for deciding, arbitrating, steering.
Between these two extremes lies a gap: the pragmatic, strategic, décision-oriented training that treats
AI as what it has actually become in the modern enterprise - a lever of transformation that is steered
like a marketing budget, an HR plan, or a growth strategy.

This training fills that gap. It will not teach you how to build a machine learning model. It will teach you
how to ask the right questions, identify the real use cases, arbitrate between vendor proposals, build a
credible roadmap in front of your ExCom, measure what actually matters, write an internal policy that
protects your organization, and position yourself as the AI référence in your sector. In other words, it
will make you an AI-literate exécutive, capable of steering your organization's'AI strategy with the same
authority you bring to any other topic.

le Head of Digital, the persona this training is built around, is the Head of Digital of a mid-sized company with
800 employees. Her CEO has just given her a broadened mandate: make AI a compétitive advantage
within the next 18 months. She is not technical. She has no CTO who speaks her language. She has a
budget, a roadmap to write, an ExCom expecting results, and a market saturated with vendors promising
everything and nothing. This training is built for le Head of Digital, and for every other le Head of Digital who will read these
pages.

2 Module 01 - AI for décision-makers: demystification


2.1 Module objectives
By the end of this module, you will be able to distinguish what artificial intelligence actually does
from what vendors claim it does, explain in less than three sentences the key concepts (LLM, agent,
RAG, automation, fine-tuning) to a non-technical interlocutor, identify the five most recurrent lies in AI
commercial proposals, and ask any vendor the questions that reveal whether they genuinely master the
subject or whether they are reciting a script.


1.1 - The basic vocabulary, finally explained without jargon
Artificial intelligence suffers from a vocabulary problem. The same words describe very différent things,
and vendors exploit that ambiguity to sell more expensively what is cheaper, or to promise what they
cannot deliver. Before arbitrating, you must fix the definitions. You do not need to know them in their full
technical détail. You need to know what they correspond to concretely in your organization, what they
approximately cost, and what they can or cannot do.

A LLM (Large Language Model) is a system trained on massive amounts of text to predict the most
probable next word in a sentence. This apparently simple mechanism gives birth to ChatGPT, Claude,
Gemini, Mistral. An LLM can reformulate, summarize, translate, answer questions, write emails, write
code. By default, it cannot access your company's proprietary data, it cannot browse the internet in
real time without a dedicated tool, and it has no memory between two conversations. All of that can be
added, but each is an additional layer that costs more.

An AI agent is an LLM to which the ability to execute actions has been granted - send an email, query
a database, fill a form, call an API. The agent is not a rigid program following a script: it decides itself,
step by step, the next action to take in order to achieve a goal you gave it. An agent can process an
invoice end-to-end, qualify a lead, organize a calendar. The boundary between a sophisticated agent
and a simple automated workflow is sometimes blurry, and that is exactly where vendors often oversell.

RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Génération) is the technique that allows an LLM to access your company's
proprietary documents. Instead of answering only with its général knowledge, the LLM first searches
your internal documents (contracts, procedures, knowledge bases, emails) for relevant passages, then
formulates its answer while relying on them. RAG is what turns a generic chatbot into an expert assistant
for your company. Its deployment requires a data-quality effort that is often underestimated.

Automation is the ancestor of AI and still thrives in most real-world use cases. To automate is to execute
a predefined sequence of steps without human intervention. AI adds the ability to handle unforeseen
cases, interpret unstructured data (emails, scanned documents, conversations), and adapt over time. In
many processes, the real answer is a mix: automate what is routine, and add AI only at complex décision
points.

Fine-tuning is the operation of partially retraining an existing model on data specific to your organization
so that it adopts your tone, your vocabulary, your way of handling a type of document. Fine-tuning is
often presented as the magical solution to every personalization problem. In reality, in most cases, a
well-documented RAG achieves the same results for ten times less money.

1.2 - What AI really does vs. what vendors claim




The gap between what AI actually does well today and what vendors promise is where most AI budgets
are lost. The real, reliable, productive capabilities are those related to language processing: reading,
understanding, summarizing, reformulating, classifying. Correctly deployed, these capabilities can free
up ten to forty percent of your teams' time on document processing, lead qualification, and operational
writing. That is a massive, measurable, replicable gain.

What AI does not do well, on the other hand, is making complex strategic décisions that commit the
company, understanding your business context without having been explicitly fed it via documents or
configuration, operating with zero human supervision on subjects with legal, financial, or reputational
risk, and guaranteeing zero errors. Any LLM can hallucinate - that is, invent a plausible but false piece
of information. No technology today eliminates that risk entirely. A good AI system is one that makes
such errors rare, detectable, and recoverable, not one that pretends never to produce any.

3 Module 02 - Mapping your organization's'AI opportunities


3.1 Module objectives
By the end of this module, you will be able to facilitate a "5 priority processes" workshop with your
exécutive committee or extended team, calculate a realistic AI return on investment without needing a
data scientist, distinguish a real opportunity from a passing fad, and produce an AI Opportunity Canvas
that will serve directly as the foundation for your roadmap.


2.1 - The trap of imported use cases
The first mistake of executives starting their AI strategy is to begin with use cases seen elsewhere.
They attended a conference, read an article, saw a demo, and they come back with a conviction: "we
need a customer chatbot", "we need churn prediction", "we need a sales assistant". These use cases
are not necessarily bad - they can even be excellent - but they do not come from an analysis of your
organization. They come from analysis of someone else's.

A solid AI strategy never starts with the solution. It starts with the problem. And the most profitable
problems to solve with AI in your organization are almost never those on display in keynotes. They are
mundane, invisible, repetitive, and buried in your teams' daily work: manual processing of a supplier
record, reclassification of a miscategorized support ticket, chasing an overdue invoice, preparing a sales
meeting with a prospect. That is where AI saves hours, days, weeks - measurably and durably.


2.2 - The "5 priority processes" workshop
This workshop is facilitated in one half-day with six to twelve participants from différent departments.
It produces a prioritized list of five processes to target first in your AI strategy, along with a rough ROI
estimate for each.




 10 / 39
 CAI O ACADE MY — AI ST RATE GY WI T HO U T C OD E TRAC K




Phase 1 - Broad inventory. Each participant lists, silently on sticky notes, every repetitive process they
can identify within their scope, regardless of apparent importance. The goal is quantity, not quality. A
team of ten usually produces between sixty and one hundred twenty candidate processes. It is critical
at this step to filter no idea, even the ones that look trivial. The most profitable processes are often those
nobody thinks of spontaneously.

Phase 2 - Scoring on three criteria. Each process is rated on three criteria, each from 1 to 5. Volume
measures frequency and repetitiveness: a process executed one thousand times per month scores 5,
a quarterly process scores 1. Pain measures the current frustration, error rate, or delay generated: a
process constantly producing rework scores 5, a smoothly running process that simply takes time scores
2. AI feasibility measures the technical ease of handling the process with AI: a purely textual process
with well-structured data scores 5, a process requiring subtle human judgment or missing data scores
1.

Phase 3 - Challenge of the top 10. The ten processes with the highest weighted score are subjected
to a collective stress test. For each, the team answers five questions: who would lose time again if we
removed this process? How many hours per week does this process actually consume? What data is
required for AI to handle it, and do we have it? What is the risk if AI makes a mistake? Who will be the
operational sponsor of the deployment?

Phase 4 - Selection of the top 5. The team selects by consensus five processes among the ten
challenged. Consensus matters: a divisive process is rarely the right first project, even with the highest
theoretical score, because its deployment will hit internal resistance that will compromise ROI.

2.4 - Calculating AI ROI without being a data scientist
Calculating an AI return on investment is nothing like rocket science. It rests on basic arithmetic, and
the main difficulty is not mathematical: it is honesty. Most AI ROI calculations presented in arbitration
committees are inflated, either out of naivety or out of a désiré to get the project approved. A good ROI
is a conservative ROI. Here is the five-line method.




 12 / 39
 CAI O ACADE MY — AI ST RATE GY WI T HO U T C OD E TRAC K




Gross gains. Count the time your teams currently spend on the process, multiplied by their fully loaded
hourly cost (salary × ~1.5 to account for indirect costs). Add, if relevant, the additional revenue directly
derived from the optimized process (for example, extra sales from better lead qualification).

Net gains. Apply a forty-percent haircut to gross gains. Why? Because no AI deployment reaches its full
potential. Some users never adopt the tool, some edge cases are poorly handled and fall back to human
processing, and supervision itself consumes time. A realistic one-year adoption rate is sixty percent of
theoretical. This conservatism will save your credibility at review time.

Full costs. Do not count only the license. Add the initial intégration (often underestimated by a factor of
two), training (about two days per user accumulated over the year), permanent human supervision (at
least ten percent of the freed-up time goes back to quality control during the first twelve months), and
infrastructure costs where applicable.

Net ROI and payback. Net ROI is simply the différence between net gains and full costs. Payback is
the number of months before cumulative net gains surpass cumulative full costs. A healthy AI project
shows payback under eighteen months. Beyond that, the probability that shifting priorities kill the project
mid-flight becomes too high.

4 Module 03 - Building your AI roadmap


4.1 Module objectives
By the end of this module, you will be able to produce a one-page AI roadmap understandable by
your ExCom, estimate an annual AI budget without depending on your IT department, anticipate and
neutralize internal resistance (IT, HR, middle management), and defend your roadmap in committee with
prepared answers to the most frequent objections.


3.1 - Why the one-page roadmap is the only one that survives
In ninety percent of organizations where I have been able to observe AI stratégies in action, there
existed a thirty- to eighty-page AI strategic document, commissioned from a consulting firm or produced
internally, that served as référence at kickoff, then was progressively forgotten from the third month
onward. The document was perfect on paper, exhaustive, well-structured, but no one consulted it
anymore. In its place, on the CEO's desk, you would find an informal one-page version, scribbled in
meetings or mentally recomposed, which was consulted daily.

The lesson is clear. The format that actually guides décisions is the one that fits within your immédiate
field of vision. Your goal is not to produce the most comprehensive document - it is to produce the
most-consulted document. That imposes a brutal constraint: one page, no more. This constraint does
not impoverish strategic work; it forces it toward the essentials. All the mapping work done in Module
02 feeds this page, but the page itself does not reproduce that work - it synthesizes it.


3.2 - The six mandatory sections of the 1-page AI Roadmap

 CAI O ACADE MY — AI ST RATE GY WI T HO U T C OD E TRAC K




Section 1 - Strategic ambition in two lines. This section answers: why are we investing in AI now, and
what must we gain in eighteen months? It is not a generic intent statement. It cites a number. "Reduce
client file processing time by twenty percent by December 2027, to redeploy the team on high-value
cases." This kind of phrasing closes the door on philosophical debates and opens the one on operational
arbitration.

Section 2 - The five priority use cases. Direct carry-over from Module 02. Each case is presented on
a single line with its name, estimated ROI, delivery deadline, and business sponsor. This list will be
challenged in committee. Prepare to justify every choice and, especially, every exclusion.

Section 3 - Total budget for the current year. One line with the total amount and its breakdown into at
most three lines: licenses and tools, external services, training and change management. The ExCom
does not read a fifteen-line budget. It reads a four-line one.

Section 4 - Team and sponsors. For each priority use case, a business sponsor identified by first name
and function, and an operational lead carrying delivery. If a box is empty, the project does not yet exist.

Section 5 - The three major risks. Risks, not hypothetical problems. Three concrète risks, each with
its countermeasure. Typical examples: regulatory risk (countermeasure: AI Act audit in June), adoption
risk (countermeasure: training and field coaching plan), vendor risk (countermeasure: twelve-month exit
clause).

Section 6 - The three success indicators. Three, no more. These are the ones you will présent every
quarter. They are measurable, dated, and have an owner. Examples: average case processing time
(owner: COO), daily adoption rate of AI tools (owner: CHRO), realized savings in euros (owner: CFO).

3.5 - Managing internal resistance
No AI roadmap passes unscathed through the first two quarters of deployment without meeting three
categories of internal resistance. Anticipating them and preparing countermeasures is part of strategic
work. Ignoring this dimension condemns your roadmap, even if it is technically excellent.

IT / CIO resistance. The CIO legitimately fears loss of control over the information system, the
proliferation of ungoverned SaaS tools ("shadow AI"), security and GDPR risks, and sometimes the
perception that the AI program is a bypass of their authority. The countermeasure is to involve the
CIO from roadmap design as co-owner, define with them a list of authorized and forbidden tools,
formally assign technical governance to them, and give them a dedicated section in the monthly steering
committee. The allied CIO is your greatest asset. The opposing CIO can block everything.

HR and managerial resistance. Employees fear for their jobs, sometimes openly, more often in silence.
Middle managers fear seeing their rôle redefined, their teams shrunk, their legitimacy eroded. The
countermeasure goes through honest communication from the outset: AI removes tasks, not positions,
and productivity gains will be reinvested in new missions. This promise has value only if kept. A single
layoff communicated as "a consequence of AI" can destroy two years of trust. Commit formally, in
writing, to the principle of redeployment rather than workforce reduction, at least for the first eighteen
months.

ExCom resistance. Paradoxically, the most dangerous resistance often comes from the exécutive
committee itself. Other directors perceive the AI program as a shadow zone in their scope, a source
of opacity in their budget, a reputational risk they do not control. The countermeasure is radical
transparency: a monthly dashboard accessible to all ExCom members, quarterly reviews with hard
numbers, and a "zero surprise" policy in which any deviation over ten percent is communicated
immediately.

5.1 Module objectives
By the end of this module, you will be able to define the eight metrics every board should see each
month on its AI program, build yourself a monthly AI ROI report in under two hours without a developer,
interpret an AI dashboard to extract the right arbitration décisions, and distinguish true value-création
signals from statistical illusions or announcement effects.


4.1 - Why measurement kills or sustains your AI strategy
AI programs die from lack of measurement as much as from lack of results. This is counterintuitive. Many
executives think all they need is for projects to work for their value to become obvious. The reality is the
opposite: even an AI project that objectively creates value will be perceived as a failure by the ExCom
if no one measures the results. Conversely, a mediocre but well-measured program that displays its
modest successes continuously will earn the budget to become excellent.

Measurement serves three functions simultaneously. It serves to steer: it tells you what to continue,
what to stop, what to double down on. It serves to communicate: it turns intuition into proof, and proof
into budgetary legitimacy. It serves to learn: without measurement, the same mistakes repeat, the same
successes fail to replicate. An AI program without measurement lasts six to twelve months before being
progressively defunded. An AI program with rigorous measurement reinforces itself year after year.


4.2 - The eight board metrics




These eight metrics form a minimal and sufficient set. Fewer than eight hides a critical dimension. More
than eight drowns the board in operational détails beyond its level. Each metric is reported monthly with
its previous month's value, its annual target, and a traffic-light indicator (green, amber, red) that can be
interpreted instantly.

Cumulative net ROI. Euros saved or earned since program start, minus cumulative full costs. This
indicator crosses from red to green at a précise moment in the cycle, called breakeven. Before
breakeven, your rôle is to reassure on trajectory. After, your rôle is to guard against complacency.

Effective payback. Number of effective months between the launch of a use case and the moment its
cumulative gains exceed its cumulative costs. Systematic comparison with the initial estimate. A gap
under twenty percent stays within normal; above fifty percent signals a steering problem.




 20 / 39
 CAI O ACADE MY — AI ST RATE GY WI T HO U T C OD E TRAC K




Active adoption rate. Percentage of target users using the AI tool at least once a week. Critical threshold:
sixty percent. Below, promised gains will never materialize. Above, the project is on a healthy trajectory.

AI tools NPS. Net recommendation score of AI tools by their users. Negative or near-zero NPS is a
warning signal, even when other metrics are green: adoption will degrade in the coming months.

Detected error rate. Monthly count of hallucinations or serious errors flagged, proportional to total
volume processed. A serious error is one that would have had business, legal, or reputational
consequences had it not been intercepted by a human.

Human supervision time. Cumulative weekly hours spent supervising, correcting, validating AI outputs.
This metric must decrease over time without falling to zero. Too-rapid decrease signals loosened quality
control; zero decrease signals a system that is not improving.

Security incidents. Monthly count of incidents: data leaks, unauthorized access, non-compliant use.
Zero is the only acceptable target. One incident automatically triggers a governance review.

DPIA score and AI Act compliance. Percentage of use cases currently in production with an up-to-date
DPIA and documented AI Act risk classification. One hundred percent is the target. Any non-compliant
use case must be documented or paused.


4.3 - Building your monthly ROI report in two hours
Most executives think an AI ROI report requires a data scientist, a developer, and three weeks of work.
False. The monthly ROI report can be built in two hours with a standard spreadsheet, provided the base
data is collected continuously by operational sponsors. Here is the method in five steps.

Step 1 (thirty minutes). Collect adoption numbers from each use case sponsor. Send a standardized
template in the last week of the month with five fields to fill: weekly active user count, AI processing
volume, estimated human time saved, detected error count, and any incidents. Standardization is key:
a template that changes each month never gets filled.

Step 2 (twenty minutes). Update the consolidated financial sheet. Add monthly gains (time saved × fully
loaded hourly rate), subtract monthly costs (licenses, services, infrastructure), and report the monthly
and cumulative net ROI in the main table. This step is mechanical if step 1 data is well collected.

Step 3 (thirty minutes). Analyze anomalies. For every metric deviating more than twenty percent from
expected trajectory, write a line of explanation. Analysis matters more than numbers themselves: a board
does not want to read tables, it wants to read interpretations.

Step 4 (twenty minutes). Draft the synthesis in at most three paragraphs: what works, what does not,
what happens next month. Three paragraphs, no more. The discipline of brevity forces prioritization,
and prioritization is the essence of steering.

Step 5 (twenty minutes). Format the visual render. A single one- or two-page document with the
eight-metric table, three synthesis paragraphs, and optionally a ROI trend chart. No fancy slides. No
useless charts. A two-page report that gets read beats a forty-slide deck no one consults, a thousand
times over.

5.1 - The European AI Act in eight minutes
The AI Act, adopted by the European Union in 2024 and progressively entering into force between 2025
and 2027, is the world's first transversal legal framework for artificial intelligence. Its guiding principle
is easy to understand but structuring for your organization: the higher the risk an AI application poses
to people's fundamental rights, safety, or health, the stricter the obligations it must comply with. This
risk-based classification is the core of the text.




Unacceptable risk. Outright forbidden in Europe are AI applications that cognitively manipulate people,
that socially score individuals as in certain foreign systems, that conduct mass biometric recognition in
public spaces without a judicial framework, or that exploit the vulnerability of specific populations such
as children. Your organization is probably one hundred percent outside this category, but it is useful to
know it exists in order to respond in committee if the question arises.

High risk. Subject to strict obligations - exhaustive technical documentation, formalized human
oversight, déclaration to the competent authority, training-data quality management, production
monitoring - are AI systems used in eight sensitive domains: human resources (recruitment, evaluation,
promotion), credit and insurance access, justice and law enforcement, éducation, critical infrastructure,
health, migration, and democratic processes. If your company deploys an AI tool in any of these domains,
even experimentally, you fall into this category and your obligations are significant. The compliance cost
per high-risk system typically lies between fifty thousand and two hundred thousand euros in the first
year.

Limited risk. The majority of enterprise AI use cases - customer chatbots, internal assistants, content

transparency-based: clearly inform users they are interacting with an AI, label AI-generated content,
and allow users to report errors. These obligations are low-cost but non-negotiable.

Minimal risk. Anti-spam filters, basic product recommendation systems, AI in video games. No specific
obligation beyond common law. Most of your use cases in years one and two will fall under categories

5.3 - Drafting an enterprise AI usage policy
The AI usage policy is the internal document that governs who can use AI in your organization, for
what, with what data, under what conditions. Without this document, three risks materialize: employees
inputting confidential data into unsecured external tools, AI tools proliferating without coordination, and
operational décisions taken on the basis of unvalidated AI outputs that expose the organization to errors.

A good AI policy runs between ten and twenty pages. It comprises eight sections covering the essentials
without ever slipping into needless legalism.




 25 / 39
 CAI O ACADE MY — AI ST RATE GY WI T HO U T C OD E TRAC K




Section 1 - Preamble and scope. Two pages maximum. States why this policy exists, to whom it
applies, to which types of AI tools it refers, its effective date, and its planned revision date. This section
normalizes the document: it makes it a standard policy, not a manifesto.

Section 2 - Fundamental principles. One page. Five to seven guiding principles that steer every AI
décision in the organization. Examples: systematic human oversight for impact décisions, privacy by
default, transparency to internal and external users, priority on data quality, reversibility of automated
décisions, fairness and absence of discriminatory bias.

Section 3 - Governance and rôles. Two pages. Describes who decides what: who validates a new use
case, who approves a new vendor, who runs incident management, who holds authority over the policy
itself. The rôle of CAIO or AI lead is formalized, with explicit scope boundaries.

Section 4 - Authorized and forbidden tools. One continuously updated page. Named list of AI tools
employees may or may not use. Revised quarterly. Without it, "shadow AI" takes hold and becomes
uncontainable.

Section 5 - Data and use classification. Two pages. Defines which data can pass through which tools.
Typical example: public data everywhere, internal data in approved tools only, identifying customer data
only in EU-hosted tools, strategic data never in any external tool.

Section 6 - User obligations. Two pages. Clear, jargon-free list of what each employee must and must
not do: report detected AI errors, not enter sensitive data, verify outputs before décisions, complète the
mandatory annual training, sign an annual commitment to policy compliance.

Section 7 - Incident management. Two pages. Protocol in case of an AI incident: who is notified, within
what delay, through which escalation channels. This section must be operational - a dedicated phone
number or email, an alert template, a guaranteed response delay.

Section 8 - Sanctions and revisions. One page. Specifies consequences of policy breaches, from a
formal warning up to disciplinary sanction depending on severity. Sets the frequency of policy revision
itself (annual minimum).


5.4 - AI incident management protocol

An AI incident is a situation where an AI system has produced, or could have produced, an unwanted
consequence for the organization, its customers, or its employees. Three severity levels must be
distinguished in advance, so as not to improvise under pressure when an incident hits.

Level 1 - Minor incident. An AI error detected before producing any real consequence, or whose impact
is contained in non-critical internal use. Treatment: reporting by the discoverer, written report within
forty-eight hours by the operational sponsor, quarterly review by the steering committee.

Level 2 - Significant incident. An AI error having produced a notable internal consequence, or an
incorrect output having reached a customer without serious harm. Examples: automatic email sent to
wrong recipient, erroneous commercial proposal corrected before acceptance, document classification
error affecting a manager-level décision. Treatment: CEO informed within twenty-four hours, action plan
within five business days, monthly review until closure.

Level 3 - Major incident. Personal or strategic data leak, AI décision having caused harm to a
customer or employee, embarrassing public AI output, identified discriminatory bias, proven regulatory
non-compliance. Treatment: immédiate ExCom convened, crisis cell activated, external communication
prepared within twelve hours, CNIL notification within seventy-two hours if personal data involved,
weekly review until full closure.

6.1 - The perfect vendor brief
An AI vendor brief is a three- to five-page document sent to candidate vendors before the first
commercial meeting even takes place. Its purpose is not to describe the expected solution - it does
not exist yet, this is exactly what you are asking the vendor to design. Its purpose is to describe the
problem to solve, the context, the constraints, and the criteria against which proposals will be evaluated.
A good brief saves weeks of useless meetings by ruling out, at first read, vendors who do not match
your framing.




Section 1 - Organization context. One page. Company size, sector, current AI maturity level, existing
tech stack, history of previous AI projects (successful or failed), internal culture toward innovation. A
vendor reading this page instantly knows whether their DNA matches.

Section 2 - Problem to solve. One page. A single précise use case, described by the problem, not by
the solution. Not "we want a chatbot" but "we want to reduce our level-1 customer support response
time, currently twelve hours on average, to under two hours, on a volume of three thousand tickets
per month". This phrasing opens the door to multiple possible solutions, letting you evaluate vendor
creativity and relevance.

Section 3 - Non-negotiable constraints. Half a page. Budget ceiling, maximum timeline, security
requirements (ISO 27001, EU hosting, AI Act compliance), transparency requirements (access to code,
documentation), contractual requirements (reversibility, ownership of data and models). Vendors who
cannot meet these constraints disqualify themselves - exactly what you want.

Section 4 - Expected deliverables. One page. Three-phase structure: a short and inexpensive POC
(proof of concept) phase, a pilot phase on a limited scope with explicit stopping criteria, and a rollout
phase conditional on the success of the previous two. This three-step structure is your best protection
against projects that drag on indefinitely.

Section 5 - Evaluation criteria with explicit weighting. Half a page. You publish in advance how you
will score proposals. Example: problem understanding twenty percent, solution quality twenty-five
percent, project team maturity twenty percent, comparable références fifteen percent, cost and pricing

Section 6 - Process timeline. Four lines. Proposal submission deadline, pitch dates, selection date,
desired kickoff date. A published and respected timeline filters out vendors who cannot honor a
commitment.

Section 7 - Expected response format. Half a page. Maximum page count, mandatory sections, CVs

7.4 - Pitching to speak at your industry's conferences
A sector-conference talk - industry trade show, firm's'annual conference, keynote at a business event
- produces in one hour what twenty LinkedIn posts produce in one year. It confers status, creates
secondary content (replays, articles, interviews), multiplies your visibility in a qualified circle. Executives
who underestimate this lever miss a major accelerator.

Getting a talk requires a structured approach. Conference organizers constantly seek speakers, but they
seek credible, differentiated profiles capable of holding thirty to sixty minutes of content without being
boring. A successful talk pitch fits on one page and comprises six éléments.

An incisive title. Not "AI in sector X", but "Why the three most obvious AI use cases in sector X are also
the riskiest". The title promises tension, perspective, an angle.

A three-sentence pitch. The promise of the talk, what the audience will concretely take away, why
you are the legitimate person to address the topic. No "I will talk about", but "at the end of this talk,
participants will be able to distinguish…".

A five-point outline. No more. Organizers must be able to visualize the structure immediately without
reading two pages of détails.

A one-hundred-fifty-word speaker bio. Results-oriented, not career-oriented. "le Head of Digital led the AI rollout
of three French mid-sized companies, generating cumulative ROI of three million euros in eighteen
months" rather than "le Head of Digital holds an MBA from school X and has held positions at Y and Z".

A verifiable social proof. One or two prior-talk références with links, or failing that, written articles,
podcasts, viral posts. If you have zero external social proof, start with smaller formats: internal webinars,
one-hour school talks, association panels. Build the CV before aiming for the keynote.

7.7 - Three personal branding pitfalls to avoid
Pitfall 1 - Generic discourse. Posts that could be signed by any other exécutive. Countermeasure: take a
stance, cite your context, share your numbers. An exécutive who only tells generalities is not a référence,
she is a curator.

Pitfall 2 - Mimicking American gurus. Copying the formats, tonalities, claims of American AI
consultants. Countermeasure: find your own voice, often more sober, more substantiated, more
European. The European market rewards rigor and honesty, not hyperbole.

Pitfall 3 - Over-investment at the expense of operations. Spending ten hours a week posting and
neglecting the internal roadmap. Countermeasure: cap personal branding time at three weekly hours
maximum. External credibility collapses if internal execution does not follow.
