# 800% in two years: the silent pivot that manufactures Chief AI Officers

> Line-by-line analysis of a business plan for a school that does not teach AI — it manufactures the executive role that will run the AI systems inside companies. Fifty minutes to understand why the window closes in eighteen months.

## 0:00 — Hook (15-30s)

Eight hundred percent. That is the search volume curve for the term "Chief AI Officer" between 2023 and 2025. Not ten percent. Not fifty. Eight hundred. And this is not a linguistic trend. It is the scream of a role that has just been born, and that no one yet knows how to train. The window to install oneself as the reference point for this role closes in eighteen months. What follows is the exact mechanism of that shift.

## 0:30 — Reframing (1-3 min)

The dominant narrative about AI is a technical narrative. You are told that you need to learn prompts. Master Claude. Build agents. Understand the stack. That narrative is false — not because these things do not exist, but because they are not the subject. They are the execution layer of a subject we refuse to name.

The real subject is organizational. A company that wants to integrate AI needs someone who can read its organization as a system, identify zones of asymmetry, decide where AI creates a competitive advantage, and arbitrate between automation and augmentation. That someone is neither the CTO, nor the CEO, nor the external consultant. It is a role whose name is not yet stabilized, even though the market is increasingly converging on one word: Chief AI Officer.

Companies are looking for this profile. They do not yet know how to qualify it. There is no certification. No school. No standard. The question posed by the business plan we are about to analyze is not "how to become a CAIO" — it is "who will manufacture the CAIOs". Because whoever defines the job description of a role under active structuring captures the editorial sovereignty of that role for ten years.

The document I am going to break apart over the next hour is not a school plan. It is a plan for a silent pivot. An ecosystem that manufactures, certifies, places, and supplies the systems used by a role that does not yet exist. And the mechanism is more interesting than the numbers.

## 3:00 — The role that has just been born

Let us start from the base. A CTO manages infrastructure. He answers "can this be built". A CEO sets direction. He answers "where are we going". Between the two, there is a functional gap that did not need to be filled as long as technology progressed linearly. As soon as a technology starts to restructure the way humans produce value — and AI does exactly this — the gap becomes cavernous.

The Chief AI Officer is that functional plug. He operates at the intersection of three domains that never coexist inside a single classical role. Business strategy, because he decides where AI creates an advantage. Organizational psychology, because he understands how humans adopt — or sabotage — intelligent systems. And technological culture, with enough depth to arbitrate, but not so much as to get lost in implementation. A CAIO does not necessarily code. He does not prompt either. He arbitrates.

This triple competence is rare. Not because it is hard to acquire — it is not, fundamentally. Because it corresponds to no existing training path. A former CTO is too deep in the technical. An ex-consultant is too far from the real. A PM is too product-focused. No classical path produces this profile. It must be manufactured deliberately.

And that is where the window opens. When a role has no classical production path, the first actors to define its job description become the reference points for the role. Not through intrinsic quality. Through temporal structure. Whoever arrives first with a coherent grammar, a precise vocabulary, a certification path, and a registry of verified profiles — that actor captures the definition of the role.

In ten years, the question "what is a good CAIO" will be settled by three or four references. Those references are being built right now. Not in two years. Now. And it is this asymmetry — between the speed of the role's emergence and the slowness of traditional institutions — that opens the door, briefly, to an independent ecosystem capable of installing the grammar before the big players.

We need to insist on the nature of this triple competence, because the misunderstanding is total in the market. Many actors think a CAIO is a CTO who has read some articles about LLMs. Others think a CAIO is a transformation consultant with superficial knowledge of the stack. Both readings are false — and they miss the same thing. The CAIO is not the sum of existing competences. He is the arbiter of a triangle that did not exist before. When you add a new vertex to a triangle, you do not create a quadrilateral. You create a new triangle, with new internal tensions, new diagonals, new centers of gravity. The CAIO manages those tensions. A CTO arbitrating AI without the psychological-organizational dimension produces orphan infrastructure. A consultant arbitrating AI without technological depth produces recommendations that do not survive implementation. A PM arbitrating AI without strategic business vision produces isolated features. All three fail for the same structural reason: they are missing one side of the triangle. The CAIO is the only role designed around all three sides simultaneously.

## 7:00 — The eighteen-month window

The window has a precise calendar. You have to read it carefully to understand why the timing is neither obvious nor eternal.

First sequence: 2025-2026. The market is forming. Companies are looking for CAIOs but do not know how to qualify them. Searches around the term have exploded by eight hundred percent between 2023 and 2025. Job postings appear. Published salaries hover between one hundred fifty and four hundred thousand euros annually depending on company size. No one has yet created the certification. This is the moment where an actor can install the grammar of the role without frontal competition.

Second sequence: 2027-2028. The market densifies. Coursera, Google, Microsoft through LinkedIn Learning will mass-produce generic certifications around AI Leadership. These are catalogs. Not ecosystems. But their distribution power is crushing. Any training at sixty-seven euros per month finds itself in direct competition with curricula sold for twenty-nine ninety-nine. The only possible defense at that moment is called: already owning proprietary positioning, an installed base of certified profiles, and an operational placement network. Without those three assets, one disappears.

Third sequence: 2029-2030. The value is no longer in content. The value is in the network. Whoever controls the community of qualified CAIOs, and connects them with companies looking to hire, owns the durable asymmetry. Tools will have changed three times. The humans who were trained will remain. The Registry — the database of certified profiles crossed with the database of subscribed companies — becomes the principal asset. Not the training. The network.

That three-step sequence draws an inverted trajectory. In 2025, one sells content. In 2027, one sells belonging. In 2030, one sells a network. The initial training is only the acquisition of the right to enter the network. If you create your school in 2027, you will be selling content while the early actors are already selling network. You will be mechanically two economic cycles behind.

This is why the eighteen-month window is not a marketing formula. It is the exact description of an asset shift. What is sold today at the price of content will be sold in four years at the price of the network. Whoever is not in the first wave will pay later to enter. Or will never enter. And this differential — between the one who writes the grammar and the one who recites it — is the hidden engine of every elite transition. Editorial elite status on an executive role is not bought back at content prices. It is captured at the moment of formation, or it is not captured at all.

There is an exact reference to this mechanism in the English-speaking space. Reforge, in the United States, sells executive trainings around product roles for approximately two thousand dollars per year. They installed their grammar six years ago around the Head of Growth role. Today, one cannot credibly claim a Head of Growth position in a scale-up without passing through their vocabulary. Reforge certification has become an implicit standard. They do not have the largest school. They have the first. And primacy in an executive role under formation is worth more than scale in a mature role. The business plan we are analyzing does not hide this reference. It names it explicitly as competition to watch — anglophone and product-oriented, which leaves intact the francophone and AI-oriented niche. A niche whose French, Belgian, Swiss market is underserved on this precise positioning, and which, combined with the timing of the role's emergence, forms the exact definition of an exploitable window.

## 12:00 — The asymmetry of the five avatars

The business plan identifies five target profiles. It does not treat them equally. That inequality is the second silent pivot of the model, and it deserves close attention.

First profile: the CTO of a SaaS, thirty-seven years old, Lyon or Paris. He can code AI but does not know how to structure a strategy or pitch to his board. He fears losing value to AI-native profiles aged twenty-five. His twelve-month LTV is nine thousand five hundred euros — three thousand five hundred for the training, plus five hundred per month of mastermind. His main channel is LinkedIn. Priority number one.

Second profile: the AI Consultant, thirty-eight years old, anywhere. He improvises his missions without methodology. He loses deals to certified profiles. His day rate stagnates while demand explodes. His LTV is the highest on the matrix: twenty-one thousand five hundred euros. Three thousand five hundred of training, plus fifteen hundred per month of Premium mastermind. The perfect avatar for the Premium upsell. Priority number two.

Third profile: the SME CEO, fifty-one years old, regional. She sees her competitors moving on AI, she has no internal resources, she has been poorly advised before. Her LTV exceeds thirty thousand euros — but through a different channel. She does not buy training. She buys B2B workshops at twenty-five thousand euros, or she buys the placement service of a certified CAIO through the Registry. Priority number three, activable from month six onward via referral.

Fourth profile: the Head of Digital, forty-three years old, large enterprise. Her CEO asks her to do something with AI. She has a budget but depends on vendors who keep her in the dark. LTV of five thousand euros — six months of community training at sixty-seven euros, then core training at three thousand five hundred with a three-installment plan. Priority number four.

Fifth profile: the Ambitious Engineer, twenty-eight years old, fully remote. Technical skills but no strategic legitimacy. Consumes AI content constantly but builds nothing monetizable. Wants to become a CAIO in twelve months. His LTV is the lowest: three thousand eight hundred euros. But his volume is the highest. And above all, he is the most effective ambassador — he speaks to an entire generation of engineers on the rise. Priority five in value. Priority one in distribution.

This matrix is not demographic. It is strategic. The CTO and the Consultant pay for growth. The SME CEO pays for high-end revenue. The Head of Digital pays for depth. The Engineer pays for audience. Ignoring this hierarchy means trying to speak to all five simultaneously starting in month one. Guaranteed result: message dilution, zero conversion. The business plan formulates it as a hard principle: beta phase, fifteen people, focus only on CTO and Consultant. Acquisition sequence: one, then two, then five. Never five at once.

That discipline is nearly impossible to hold without a strict definition of the message. And this is where per-avatar specialization plays its true role. Each training path has its productive frustration at module three or four, designed for the target avatar. The CTO does not experience the same frustration as the Consultant. The Ambitious Engineer does not experience the same frustration as the SME CEO. A single generalist path would lose them all. Five precise paths create five precise pains, five precise trajectories, five precise upsells. Personalization is not a pedagogical luxury. It is the mechanical condition of the transition from one tier to the next.

Let us look at the detail avatar by avatar. The CTO SaaS path is called AI Systems Architect Track, eight modules. The first module addresses posture: moving from code to strategy. The second teaches reading an organization as an AI system. The third describes an end-to-end AI system architecture. The fourth builds the first monitoring system in a light version. And that is exactly where the frustration appears — I want the complete system with my data — which shifts to the three thousand five hundred tier. The fifth module teaches pitching AI to a board. The following ones build the CAIO Fractional offer and plan the first ten thousand euros of revenue.

The Head of Digital path is called AI Strategy Without Code Track, seven modules. It is designed for decision-makers who will never code. Demystification, mapping opportunities, roadmap, ROI measurement, governance, vendor management, positioning as an industry reference. The frustration appears at module four: I want to build my own dashboard with my data, even if I do not code. The three-installment plan of the core training allows the transition without CFO arbitration.

The AI Consultant path is called CAIO Monetization Track, eight modules. Market mapping, three-tier offer packaging, selling to non-technical executives, delivery without complex tools, selling systems rather than consulting. The frustration appears at module five: I see the model but I have no systems to resell. The transition to the core training is not bought — it imposes itself as the only way to capture the strategic premium of the role.

The Ambitious Engineer path is called From Dev to CAIO Track, eight modules. Exiting executor mode, mastering the 2025 CAIO stack, building a first functional agent, speaking business, public portfolio, getting noticed, finding the first mission, twelve-month roadmap toward serious revenue. The frustration appears at module three: I wish I had the three complete systems as a base. The six-installment plan at six hundred fifty makes the transition economically accessible even for a junior profile.

The SME CEO path is called AI Executive Decision Maker Track, only five modules, the shortest with six hours total. Ten real SME cases, investment decision, recruiting the right CAIO profile, onboarding teams, executive oversight. Its specificity: she does not move up to the core training. Her natural upsell is the Registry. The exact phrase from the plan: you want us to do it for you, we have certified profiles available starting this month. Average ticket: fifteen to thirty thousand euros. Two parallel entry points inside the same ecosystem, for two radically different purchasing movements.

## 18:00 — The three tiers of power

The economic model stacks three tiers. Each has a precise economic function. Confusing them breaks the mechanism.

Entry tier: sixty-seven euros per month, cancelable. It is the theoretical training. Five personalized paths, one per avatar. Eight modules per path. One template system in a lightweight version. Access to the community. The CAIO Brief newsletter. Quarterly content updates.

This tier has a counterintuitive function. It is not there to generate revenue. It is there to create a precise frustration, at module three or four depending on the path, that makes the transition to the next tier obvious. The business plan uses a formulation worth hearing: intentionally incomplete value. Not teasing. Not artificial retention. Real value — but with the complete system kept out. The subscriber sees the mechanism, understands what needs to be built, and runs into a soft barrier: I want the complete system with my data. The barrier is an invitation, not a lock.

Core tier: three thousand five hundred euros one-shot, lifetime access, payment plan available across three installments of twelve hundred fifty or six installments of six hundred fifty. It is the complete training. Everything in tier one included. Three complete builds: AI monitoring system, guided agent orchestration, AI ROI dashboard from scratch. All templates and systems complete. CAIO Academy certificate. Access to the CAIO Registry with visible profile.

This tier is the real product. It is designed so that each build, once resold to a client, is worth more than the training itself. An AI ROI dashboard sold to an SME client billed ten thousand euros reimburses the training in one mission. The one-shot lifetime access is the key. The client is not buying a six-month program. He is buying a resalable library of systems. The accounting difference is radical. In the buyer's head, it is not a training expense. It is an investment in resalable assets. A mental category shift that changes the psychological threshold of the purchase.

Mastermind tier: five hundred euros per month, thirty members maximum, plus a premium tier at fifteen hundred euros with ten members maximum. Two collective calls per month of ninety minutes each. Bi-monthly hot seat. Access to all new systems from the day they are released. Shared lead network between members. Private thread with response within forty-eight hours. Co-creation and review of each member's offers.

The mastermind has the subtlest function: it is the recurring backbone that stabilizes the treasury. Thirty members at five hundred euros is fifteen thousand euros per month guaranteed. The limit at thirty is intentional — it creates the scarcity that justifies the price and forbids service degradation. The premium tier, at fifteen hundred euros with ten members, adds fifteen thousand euros per month extra and installs a monthly one-on-one call, a warm intro to the Registry, and placement priority. It is the top of the pyramid, explicitly sized for the AI Consultant who pays back his monthly fee with one additional mission per month.

The three tiers are not parallel products. They are a trajectory. The free tier feeds the sixty-seven. The sixty-seven feeds the three thousand five hundred. The three thousand five hundred feeds the five hundred monthly. Each tier feeds the next — but only if the preceding tiers have been built to produce the right frustration just before the jump. A business plan that stacks three tiers without that mechanism is a catalog. This one is a trajectory. The nuance holds in one word: the transition is not the buyer's choice, it is a structural consequence of what the preceding tier has delivered.

## 25:00 — The flywheel mechanism

Around the three tiers, the business plan adds three bonus revenue streams. Each looks harmless. Taken together, they form the real moat.

First stream: systems sold individually. AI monitoring at two hundred ninety-seven euros. Agent orchestration at four hundred ninety-seven. Complete three-system pack at nine hundred ninety-seven. Distribution through Gumroad and direct sale to the community. In year three, two hundred complete packs generate one hundred ninety-nine thousand four hundred euros.

This stream is not an annex. It is the proof that the training works. Each system sold to a non-student is a live demonstration that the assets delivered in training are actually operational. It is also a bridge: the buyer of a five-hundred-euro system becomes a prospect for the three-thousand-five-hundred training. The system convinces before the training. He pays to see. Then he sees. Then he buys the training to learn to build it himself.

Second stream: B2B placement. Headhunting commission of twenty percent of the first contract plus twenty-five hundred euros fixed fee per placement. B2B workshops inside companies, target SMEs of fifty-plus employees, between eight thousand and twenty-five thousand euros per workshop. Model active from month nine onward — when fifty certified profiles are available.

This stream is pivotal. It turns training from an expense into a purchasing asset. A company looking for a fractional CAIO pays twenty-five hundred euros in fixed fees to access the Registry, then twenty percent of the first contract. On an average contract of forty-five thousand euros, that is eleven thousand five hundred euros per placement. Ten placements per year is one hundred fifteen thousand euros. And the marginal cost of this stream, once the community is in place, approaches zero. Training has already paid the acquisition cost. Placement only adds an intermediation job.

Third stream: enterprise subscription to the Registry. One thousand euros per month per company, unlimited access to certified profiles, prioritized introductions, CAIO market analytics. Year three target: thirty subscribed companies. Three hundred sixty thousand euros of recurring ARR.

This last stream is the real defensive asset. Because it is not transactional — it is subscribed. Because it creates a two-sided network effect: the more certified profiles in the Registry, the more valuable the enterprise subscription; the more subscribed companies, the more desirable the certification. Classic two-sided flywheel. And once critical mass is reached, any new competitor must replicate both sides in parallel. Which a new entrant cannot do in a reasonable time.

The ecosystem is closed. Free that qualifies. Training that certifies. Mastermind that retains. Systems that prove. Placement that monetizes. Enterprise subscription that stabilizes. Six levers that self-reinforce. No current competitor offers all six. Reforge has part — anglophone, product-oriented. Major consulting firms have part — AI workshops for executives at three to five thousand euros per day, out of reach of SMEs. LinkedIn AI creators have part — plenty of theory, little real practice, no certification. No one has everything. And it is the combination that makes the moat, not any single lever taken in isolation. A competitor who wants to replicate must manufacture six assets simultaneously. Each has its learning curve, its build cycle. The temporal window to install all six in parallel, before the market closes around it, is counted in months, not in years.

## 30:00 — The three-year trajectory

The numbers in the plan are conservative. They must be read with that caution in mind — and with the understanding that the real plan is not year one. It is the shift to year three.

Year one: two hundred fifteen thousand euros. The beta cohort of fifteen people at fifteen hundred euros lays the foundation — twenty-two thousand five hundred euros. The Founding Cohort training at three thousand five hundred sells forty-eight units — one hundred sixty-eight thousand euros. The mastermind at five hundred euros welcomes twenty members over six months — sixty thousand euros. Systems sold individually, deliberately absent: the audience is insufficient that year. Year one validates product-market fit. It must not dilute energy on marginal streams.

Year two: six hundred twenty thousand euros on the conservative line, nine hundred eighty thousand optimistic post-ramp. Training at three thousand five hundred: one hundred twenty sales, four hundred twenty thousand euros. Standard mastermind: thirty members over twelve months, one hundred eighty thousand. Premium mastermind: five members over twelve months, ninety thousand. CAIO placements: ten placements, one hundred fifteen thousand. B2B workshops: eight companies at twelve thousand euros average, ninety-six thousand. Systems sold individually: two hundred sales at four hundred euros average, eighty thousand.

Year three: one million fifty thousand conservative, one million four hundred thousand and more optimistic. Training at the stabilized price of three thousand euros: two hundred sales, six hundred thousand. Standard mastermind: thirty members twelve months, one hundred eighty thousand. Premium mastermind ten members twelve months: one hundred eighty thousand. CAIO placements thirty at five thousand average: one hundred fifty thousand. B2B workshops twenty at fifteen thousand average: three hundred thousand. Enterprise Registry subscription thirty at one thousand euros per month: three hundred sixty thousand. Complete system pack two hundred at nine hundred ninety-seven: one hundred ninety-nine thousand four hundred.

Observe the evolution of the revenue structure. In year one, eighty percent of revenue comes from training and mastermind. In year three, training revenue stagnates in relative share around fifty-six percent, while placements, workshops, and Registry subscription explode. The Registry alone goes from zero to three hundred sixty thousand in year three. It does not exist in year one. It is built in year two. It explodes in year three. This trajectory is not linear — it is compounded. Growth is not the product of proportional effort, it is the product of stacked assets that begin to self-reinforce after eighteen months.

This evolution is the economic signature of a successful ecosystem. The initial product — the training — is no longer the main product. It becomes an acquisition asset that feeds recurring assets at higher margin. Companies do not pay one thousand euros per month for content. They pay one thousand euros per month to access a profile base they cannot recruit otherwise. That base is what the training manufactured.

There lies the real mechanism of the plan. Training is not the end. It is the means of producing the inventory of profiles that justifies the enterprise subscription. The real business is the enterprise subscription. Training is the factory that makes it possible. And this inverted reading is the signature of a plan that thinks in years — not in quarters. Whoever looks only at year one sees a small school. Whoever looks at year three sees a B2B marketplace with an unassailable editorial asset.

## 37:00 — The five risks and their responses

A business plan without a devil's advocate is a draft. This one names five structural risks. Each has a structured response. Walking through them is the best way to read the balance of the model.

Risk number one: the target does not code. Real C-levels have neither the time nor the vocation to build systems. Selling "build yourself" to decision-makers means maximum friction at purchase.

Response: the main target is not the CEO. It is the CTO SaaS and the AI Consultant — two profiles who code or know what it means. Pure C-levels have paths adapted without code, oriented toward governance and oversight. Two markets, one ecosystem. The business plan refuses the option of one school for all. It lays two parallel entries. That is assumed asymmetry, not contradiction.

Risk number two: the stack becomes obsolete. Convex, Composio, Claude Code — if one of these tools pivots or disappears in eighteen months, all content is stale and credibility collapses.

Response: brand identity is the CAIO role, not the stack. The stack is an implementation detail. Quarterly content updates included in the subscription. Principle: we teach concepts, we update tools. This response is deeper than it looks. It posits that the asset to protect is editorial authority over the role, not technical expertise over a tool. A competitor can replicate the stack. He cannot replicate five years of already-posted editorial authority. If Composio disappears tomorrow, the CAIO Academy brand survives. If the brand disappears, no tool can rebuild it.

Risk number three: pricing is incoherent. Sixty-seven euros per month cannibalizes three thousand five hundred. The two tiers target similar needs at radically different prices.

Response: the sixty-seven delivers intentionally incomplete value. Each path has a precise frustration at module three or four that makes the transition obvious. This is not artificial retention — it is a natural progression. The client does not cross a price wall. He crosses a need wall. And that difference makes everything. A price wall is worked around by competitive comparison. A need wall is only bypassed by buying the solution that removes it.

Risk number four: the five hundred euros per month is empty. The promise of leads is not a value — it is a promise one does not control. Without solid content, that tier collapses quickly.

Response: the mastermind is restructured on four concrete pillars. Two calls per month. Bi-monthly hot seat. Access to updated systems. Shared lead network. The main value is not the promise of leads. It is direct access to the facilitator and to a qualified community. Leads are an emergent bonus, not a central promise. This correction matters. A mastermind that promises leads it cannot guarantee dies in six months. A mastermind that promises access and actually delivers that access creates structural retention.

Risk number five: you launch too wide too early. Five avatars simultaneously before having validated a single path is total dispersion.

Response: beta phase, fifteen people maximum, focus only on CTO and Consultant. Validate, iterate, scale. The five avatars exist in the roadmap — not in the launch. Sequence: one, two, five. That discipline is the hardest to hold. A business plan that promises five avatars starting in month one is a business plan that dies by month six. This one constrains itself to start with two. It is a voluntary restriction that installs a hard principle: do not scale complexity before the base is installed.

The five risks are real. The five responses are structurally coherent. What is striking in this document is that the responses are not reassuring. They are restrictive. The plan voluntarily constrains itself to avoid each risk. That is the signature of an operational plan — as opposed to a pitch. A pitch promises. An operational plan constrains itself.

## 43:00 — The content that precedes everything

One detail of the business plan deserves attention before closing. The content strategy for the first three months is explicitly: LinkedIn first, always. No YouTube first. No TikTok. Three posts per week. One weekly newsletter. Months one to three: zero sales, one hundred percent audience.

This discipline is counterintuitive in an era where everyone pushes video reach. The reason is precise: the priority buyer — the CTO SaaS and the AI Consultant — is on LinkedIn. He is not on TikTok. He is not looking for a school on YouTube. He is looking for a peer who speaks his language on the platform where he already does his professional scanning. Three formats follow each other. Monday, concrete case — a system built, a quantified result, a before/after. Wednesday, position statement — an assumed conviction that polarizes. Friday, stack or tool breakdown — a technical demonstration with a business conclusion.

And in parallel, three systems are built publicly. System one: AI monitoring for a fictional or real SME. System two: agent orchestration to automate an HR or commercial process. System three: AI ROI dashboard with Convex and Next.js. Each system is documented live. Each step becomes a post. Each post feeds the newsletter. The newsletter qualifies the community. The community becomes the beta cohort. The beta cohort validates product-market fit. Nothing is played blind. Everything is stacked.

This sequence is the real starter of the plan. Without the three months of authority, the beta does not sell. Without the sold beta, the Founding Cohort launch does not sell. Without the Founding Cohort, the Registry has no profiles. Without profiles, the enterprise subscription has nothing to sell. Each phase is the precondition for the next. Breaking one phase breaks the entire trajectory.

The twelve-month roadmap formalizes this sequence in four phases. Months one to three: Phase I, Authority. Passage criterion: five hundred newsletter subscribers. Months four to six: Phase II, Beta. Cohort of fifteen people at fifteen hundred euros, six weeks of live program, system three built with the cohort, fifteen testimonials and case studies collected. Passage criterion: twenty-two thousand five hundred euros collected. Months six to nine: Phase III, Launch. Official opening of the training at three thousand five hundred in Founding Cohort, first one hundred seats. Mastermind opened, thirty members maximum. Individual systems on Gumroad. Registry created and populated. First B2B contacts via the SME CEO profile. Objective: twenty mastermind members. Passage criterion: fifteen thousand euros per month recurring. Months nine to twelve: Phase IV, Flywheel. First placements via Registry. First B2B workshop in a company. Complete model review. Opening of the year two cohort waitlist. Construction of the year two plan. Year one objective: approximately two hundred fifteen thousand euros collected.

Each passage criterion is quantified. Each phase has one single priority. Not moving to the next phase without having reached the validation criterion is the hard rule of the plan. That is where the difference plays out between a plan that tells itself and a plan that executes itself. A plan that tells itself accumulates initiatives in parallel. A plan that executes itself refuses phase two until phase one is validated. It is uncomfortable. It is slow at the start. It is the only way to build an ecosystem without dispersing it.

## 47:00 — The five opening moves

The business plan closes on a list of five actions to execute within the week. I name them because their precision says something about the nature of the plan.

First move: rewrite the LinkedIn bio. Not "I do AI". Not "CTO". But a calibrated sentence: I train the Chief AI Officers of tomorrow — the profiles who will steer and build the AI systems inside companies. To execute today. The LinkedIn bio is the first editorial signal. It precedes everything else. A positioning not formulated in the LinkedIn bio does not exist — it is a private intention.

Second move: launch the newsletter The CAIO Brief on Beehiiv. Minimalist landing page. No need for a complete website. A form, a first welcome email, the link sent to twenty relevant contacts. To execute this week. The newsletter is not one channel among others. It is the primary editorial asset — the email list escapes platform algorithms, survives policy changes, transports between tools. It is the only truly owned distribution property.

Third move: document an existing system. No need to create a new one. One LinkedIn post with the before/after, tools used, quantified result. It is the first proof content. Executed this week. One piece of proof is worth more than ten arguments. Priority buyers — CTO and Consultant — filter noise in one second. What they are looking for is not a thesis. It is an artifact.

Fourth move: define the three signature systems. Those that will be built publicly over ninety days. Each becomes three things in one: LinkedIn content, training demo, product sold individually. Give each a name and a one-line pitch. Executed this week. This triple function per system is the economic key of the content strategy. Every hour of construction generates three assets. What is not built in triple-function mode costs three times more for an equivalent outcome.

Fifth move: open the Discord CAIO Network. Free. A few channels. No polishing. Manually invite twenty people from the network who match the CTO SaaS or AI Consultant avatars. The community starts now. Executed this week. The community does not wait for a finished product. It gathers around an editorial promise that is already clear. Waiting to have everything ready before opening the community means waiting for the first qualified members to be already inside another Discord.

Five moves. Each executable. None expensive. All connected. The plan closes on a sentence that summarizes its general tone: the only real risk in this plan is to think too much and not execute enough. That sentence is not a productivity slogan. It is a structural observation. The plan is designed so that execution is simpler than reflection. The inverse of the usual trap — where the more one plans, the more one delays execution. Here, every execution block generates its own feedback, which informs subsequent planning. Execution precedes planning. And for that reason, the first moves are trivial to make — and impossible to defer without betraying the very spirit of the plan.

## 50:00 — Philosophical zoom-out (2-5 min closing)

The plan itself deserves analysis. But it is not the real subject.

The real subject is what it signals. When a new executive role appears in market vocabulary with eight hundred percent growth in two years, it is not a fashion. It is the trace of a deeper restructuring. AI does not add a tool to the existing economy. It redistributes power across three layers: those who own compute, those who train the models, those who deploy them in production. The Chief AI Officer is the person who, inside a company, manages that triangle.

That triangle did not exist three years ago. It could not exist — the models were not good enough to justify a dedicated function to arbitrate them. It exists today because the asymmetry between a company that deploys AI well and a company that deploys it badly now exceeds ten percent of operational margin. That asymmetry justifies an executive seat. And an executive seat justifies a school.

This is the civilizational mechanism. Executive roles appear when a technology crosses an impact threshold. The CIO appeared in the nineties when computing moved from cost center to productivity center. The digital CMO appeared around 2010 when online marketing overtook traditional marketing. The CAIO appears now because AI has just crossed the same threshold. This is not a fashion effect. It is a threshold effect.

The distinction between a fashion effect and a threshold effect is not semantic. A fashion effect decays when attention moves elsewhere. A threshold effect is irreversible because the economic substrate beneath it has shifted. The CIO role did not disappear when the dot-com bubble burst — it institutionalized, because the underlying reality of distributed computing kept producing margin asymmetry. The digital CMO role did not disappear when individual ad platforms rose and fell — it became foundational, because the underlying reality of measurable attention kept producing competitive separation. The CAIO role will follow the same trajectory. Not because a particular model family dominates. But because the gap between a company that orchestrates its AI systems and a company that does not now compounds quarter after quarter. That gap is the threshold. It has been crossed. The role follows.

Whoever defines the grammar of this role while it is emerging installs an editorial infrastructure that will survive the tools. In ten years, Claude, GPT, and Mistral may have disappeared. The CAIO role will be stable. Just as the CIO survived Windows NT, SAP, and Oracle. The job survives its stack. Stacks are costumes. The job is a function.

This business plan is not a training plan. It is a plan for the market's capitulation toward a grammar that one actor will install. Either it will be this plan. Or it will be another. The battle is not between schools. It is between schools and the absence of schools — between a structured grammar and the editorial fog that persists today. And in that battle, fog always loses. The question is no longer whether someone will write it. The question is who.

And that is the final silent pivot of the plan. It is not written to convince the reader to join. It is written to document a writing act already in progress. An act of editorial sovereignty over an executive role in formation. If the reader finds it coherent, he joins. If he finds it incoherent, he writes his own. What is impossible is to ignore that such an act is being posed — and that the grammar of the role will stabilize around whoever writes it most precisely.

## 55:00 — CTA (closing invitation)

I am not asking you to subscribe. I am asking you to look at your organization, your career, your network, and to ask a precise question: who, around you, already holds the Chief AI Officer role without anyone having named it. That person exists. Inside your company or at your competitor. The question is not whether the role will exist. The question is who will hold it. And in ten years, when we look at the grammar installed around the CAIO, it will be the first ones who wrote it who will sign the definition. It is up to you whether you want to be part of that writing — or receive it.
